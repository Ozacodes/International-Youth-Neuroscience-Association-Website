Choose Your Category

High school students enter the High School category only. Post-secondary students and early-career trainees may enter one essay each in the Academic and General Audience categories. Anyone may add a video essay. Every essay and video is submitted separately.

IYNA-INS High School Essay

High school & secondary students

Explore the relationship between ethics, neuroscience, and society in plain language written for the general public. Build your argument on reliable primary and secondary sources — Wikipedia and similar sources are not acceptable.

  • Length: 1,000 words maximum
  • Not counted: abstract and references
  • Counted: in-text citations and footnotes
  • Language: English, single author

General Audience Essay

Post-secondary & early career

Tell a story or unpack a topic at the interface of ethics, neuroscience, and society the way a magazine or news outlet would for non-specialist readers. Essays written for previous coursework are eligible if they meet all other requirements.

  • Length: 1,000 words maximum
  • Not counted: references and disclosures
  • Language: English, single author

Academic Essay

Post-secondary & early career

Examine a current issue in neuroethics through rigorous, evidence-based argument. Submit a polished final draft of original, unpublished work — something you could send to a scholarly journal in the field.

  • Length: 2,000 words maximum
  • Not counted: references and disclosures
  • Language: English, single author

Video Essay

Open to all eligible entrants

The audio-visual equivalent of a creative, arts-based essay. Combine your own footage, voice-over, still images, music, and text into a narrative or poetic piece. Credit anything you did not produce yourself. Production quality is not judged.

  • Length: 5 to 7 minutes
  • Format: mp4 file or a public link
  • Languages: English, Spanish, French, German, Telugu, Afrikaans, KiSwahili, isiXhosa, or Yoruba — English subtitles required
  • End credits: references and disclosures, not counted in the run time

Rules & Requirements

Read all of it before you write. Submissions that miss a requirement are not reviewed.

Your essay can cover any topic in neuroethics, and should address a focused problem at the intersections of the mind and brain sciences, ethics, and law.

Example topics

Moral philosophy, moral psychology, free will, philosophy of mind, neurology, ethics of neurodegenerative illness, clinical ethics in psychiatry and neurosurgery, neurogenetics, neuroimaging, big data and neuroscience, neuro/brain stimulation, brain-computer interfaces, neuroenhancement, neurotechnology policy and regulation, neurolaw, and military applications of neurotechnology.

There are no restrictions on neuroethical topics, and creative approaches are encouraged. Reading previous winning and honorable mention essays is the fastest way to see what a competitive essay looks like.

AI tools

Large language models and generative AI are acceptable only where the work still involves a significant and original contribution from you. You may use these tools to help frame your thoughts, copy edit, translate, and typeset. Do not use them to generate content, and check carefully for distorted or hallucinated references, concepts, and claims.

If you used an AI tool at any point, you must disclose at the end of your text which tool you used and how. Do not list an AI tool as an author or co-author. AI-generated figures and tables are not permitted.

Citations

Original work is the goal, but draw on existing research to get there: primary research articles, review papers, books, and other scholarly sources. This matters most for Academic essays. Strong essays show how they relate to existing work — discussing similarities, drawing out differences, or synthesizing several sources to support a point.

Use any citation style you like, as long as you stay consistent. For example, in APA style, this source:

Gleeson, P., Cantarelli, M., Marin, B., Quintana, A., Earnshaw, M., Sadeh, S., Piasini, E., Birgiolas, J., Cannon, R. C., Cayco-Gajic, N. A., Crook, S., Davison, A. P., Dura-Bernal, S., Ecker, A., Hines, M. L., Idili, G., Lanore, F., Larson, S. D., Lytton, W. W., Majumdar, A., … Silver, R. A. (2019). Open Source Brain: A Collaborative Resource for Visualizing, Analyzing, Simulating, and Developing Standardized Models of Neurons and Circuits. Neuron, 103(3), 395–411.e5. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuron.2019.05.019

could appear in your essay as:

Researchers have recently developed the Open Source Brain platform for “sharing, viewing, analyzing, and simulating standardized models from different brain regions and species” (Gleeson et al., 2019).

“et al.” signals that the paper you are citing has multiple authors. We care far more that you cite your sources than that you follow every rule of a style perfectly.

The contest is open to any high school or secondary school student, any post-secondary student, and any postdoctoral fellow or similar early-career trainee, anywhere in the world. You must be currently enrolled, accepted to a program for a future term, or preparing to apply to graduate programs. Your education level determines your category.

High school / secondary school students

If you were in high school or an equivalent secondary program at any point between January and July 2026, submit to the High School category.

Post-secondary students and early-career trainees

Post-secondary students include anyone enrolled in a degree-granting program at the undergraduate, graduate, or professional level, as well as students at pre-university institutions such as CEGEPs, sixth form colleges, and lycées. Early-career trainees include recent graduates in postdoctoral positions, medical residents and fellows, postbaccs, judicial law clerks and articled students at law firms, research assistants, and contract or sessional lecturers in their first or second year out of graduate training.

If either description fit you at any point between January and July 2026, you may submit to the Academic or General Audience categories — up to two essays total, one per category.

Breaks, gap years, and applicants

Students on a temporary break or gap year, prospective students actively applying to programs, and students already accepted for an upcoming semester are all eligible. Follow the guidelines for whichever category best matches your most recent studies. You may not submit to the High School category if you graduated from high school before January 2026.

Professionals and former students

Professionals are not eligible. For contest purposes, anyone who is solely an employee and not currently engaged in a training program counts as a professional.

Field of study and membership

There are no restrictions on your program or area of study, as long as you meet the education level requirements and your submission meets all requirements. You do not need to be a member of the INS or IYNA to enter.

Questions about eligibility go to staff@neuroethicssociety.org.

All four categories are submitted through the online form. Review every eligibility and submission requirement before you write, and again before you upload.

Written essays

  • Submit as a doc, docx, or pdf file.
  • Use the essay format template (docx) so your cover page carries all required information and no identifying details appear anywhere else. This is what allows your essay to be anonymized for review.
  • Name your file Essay-2026-SURNAME-Category.pdf

The template's cover page asks for your essay title, name, academic institution, department or major, and academic level, plus a details block with your word count and any disclosures. An abstract is optional — it is not required for the contest, though the IYNA Journal and some other publications do ask for one, so it is worth drafting.

Video essays

  • Upload an mp4 file, or provide a link to a video publicly available on YouTube, Vimeo, Facebook, or similar.

Essays are judged on originality and structure of argument, conceptual clarity, rigor of defense, quality of writing, and conclusions, following this year's scoring rubric. Video essays are not judged on production quality.

See the scoring rubric for written essays (pdf) and video essays (pdf).

Review process

Every submission goes through anonymous review by two or more referees from the INS Student/Postdoc Committee, or by a panel of judges chosen by the IYNA Journal Editorial Team. Referees are students and postdocs from neuroscience, law, medicine, philosophy, and other areas of the sciences and humanities. All decisions are final.

Results

Winning and honorable mention submissions are emailed to all participating authors by November 2026. You can browse winners and honorable mentions from previous contests.

After the contest

Winners are warmly encouraged, but not required, to attend the upcoming INS Annual Meeting. Entering does not prevent you from later submitting an extended version of your manuscript to academic journals or other publications. You may enter in future years regardless of prior participation, though past winners cannot win again in the same category — they are still welcome to submit and may receive an honorable mention.

Winning and honorable mention essays are posted on the contest website and may be considered for publication elsewhere, at an outlet suited to the topic. Recent winning essays have been published online by the Dana Foundation, and past winners have gone on to publish in outlets such as Scientific American.

Everyone who submits to the IYNA-INS High School Neuroethics category may opt to be considered for publication in the IYNA Journal.

Entering does not prevent you from later submitting a version of your manuscript to other magazines, academic journals, or scholarly and general interest publications.

Who Runs the Contest

Two organizations, collaborating to advance neuroscience education and neuroethics research worldwide.

International Neuroethics Society

(INS)

An interdisciplinary group of scholars, scientists, clinicians, and other professionals dedicated to encouraging and inspiring research and dialogue on the responsible use of advances in brain science. People at all stages of their careers join the Society to interact, learn, and take part in discussions that further the growing field of neuroethics.

International Youth Neuroscience Association

(IYNA)

A global, youth-led nonprofit that supports and advocates for neuroscience education. The IYNA Journal publishes articles on neuroethics and other neuroscience topics, supported by a worldwide network of members across more than 100 countries.
